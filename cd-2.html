Week 1: INTRODUCTION

Course Objectives

FPA 289 is a post-modern course in music appreciation.You will develop a critical ear and advanced listening skills through the presentation of a diversity of music from many cultures, styles, and periods, in an effort to discover similarities, differences, and defining characteristics.You will build your Music Appreciation repertoire around basic style characteristics such as rhythm, harmony, melody, timbre, and texture. We will cover such general topics as form, song, political music, technology, story telling, background music, sacred music, improvisation, dance music, women in music, and others.

This is not a course, like so many other music appreciation courses, designed to create new subscribers to the local symphony orchestra. I will not be attempting to "educate" listeners in the joys of European art music from 1760-1890 (the boundaries within which many symphony orchestras define themselves). Instead, I hope to make you have a better understanding about the similarities and differences between various musical styles and types.

Furthermore, you will develop critical listening skills, or the ability to understand and discuss music on a more informed level. Maybe you will understand better about why you may like a certain style of music, and why you may not like another style as much. And in the process, I'll introduce you to some music that may have similar characteristics, which you may then also enjoy and understand better.

All this will be done without any necessary previous knowledge of music theory, music history, or even the ability to read music.

Music and the European Art-music Tradition

Most courses about music deal mainly with the European "high-art" musical tradition - the music of Bach, Beethoven, Brahms, etc. They may also include a glance at jazz and some popular music, and perhaps some music from other cultures. There are two reasons for this. Firstly, the perceived need to "educate" listeners, so that they can "appreciate" the art music from this tradition. While the European tradition has undeniably created a wealth of wonderful music, most musicians today recognize that it is only a portion of the available music in contemporary culture. While it is important to understand why Beethoven is regarded as a genius, it is equally important to understand the importance of rhythm and blues to the evolution of rock music, and even the importance of African tribal music to rock, for example.

Therefore, a course such as this will attempt to be much more inclusive about all music. However, this brings up the second reason why this type of course relies so heavily upon the European tradition - the lack of source material outside this tradition. Since about the year 800, musicians in Europe (monks for the first 700 years!) have been writing down music, and these written documents have remained with us. As a result, we can look back upon this music, and compare it to music beforehand and afterward, and thus understand its relationship.

Outside the European art-music tradition, most music is within the oral tradition. This means that it is passed down from generation to generation by teaching it firsthand, without ever writing it down. As a result, it can be changed quite dramatically with each passing, with the original versions lost forever. Therefore, we can never understand the evolution of such music directly.

In the last one hundred years, recording technology has allowed researchers to record musicians and performances, documenting them in a permanent state. This not only applies to the study of world music, but also commercial music as well. For example, the early Sun Studio recordings of Elvis Presley display his (and the producers) unique combination of rockabilly and rhythm and blues.

Music in Culture

Each culture possesses its own musical language, which reflects its own traditions, concerns, and activities. To begin to understand the music of any culture, we need to understand something of the nature of its cultural systems and the role that music plays in them. For example, this goes for the music of the Middle Ages in Europe, Hindustani music, or 1960s acid rock.

Music in Contemporary North America

Where do we hear music in today's society?

Obviously, music is all around us -

  • we hear it in our cars,
  • over loudspeakers in malls,
  • through headphones from our portable CD players,
  • on TV during commercials,
  • in movies as background.

All of these different settings have one thing in common - we are hearing recorded music. While this is one important facet of music that we will look at later in the course, let's limit our first look at music to settings that we can hear music played live - by real musicians. What are the different types of music that we hear today in North America? How do they differ?

Classical Music

An elitist activity - performances are very formal. Members of the audience sit listening very quietly until the end of each piece. Most of the music that is performed in these concerts was composed in previous centuries. Society treats the music of the past with great reverence - there is a written musical tradition stretching back over 1000 years (though most of the music is between 100 to 300 years old). Concerts often feature the same pieces by the same composers, from one concert to another, year after year.

Popular Music

Most young people listen to this music Much simpler and less formal than classical music. Still highly structured (the same from performance to performance). Often listened to in recorded and convenient formats - in personal spaces, such as their own rooms, or via personal listening (CD/MP3 players).

However, concerts are still an important part of popular music. These concerts are very informal - audiences may talk, sing, or shout - the music is often played so loudly it doesn't matter. Another important venue is the club, in which audiences dance to the music. Almost all the music is song, involving text - most texts are about love or sex. Some performers/creators are rewarded financially completely out of proportion with other members of society (for example, doctors that can save lives…)

Jazz

Performed in cafés, nightclubs, bars. Also informal - audiences talk and drink during performance. Performers improvise, changing their performances from night to night. Small audiences, performers often of African descent.

Functional Music

We would also hear live music during parades.

Such marching bands have military overtones (and a military history), and often serve to mark patriotic and community events. The same can be said for pep bands and marching bands at football games. Music is played at the beginning of sporting events (the National Anthems of the teams involved).

Many people's most direct contact with music is when music is used purely for functional means: to stir emotions during movies, television, even commercials.

Summary

  • The use of music primarily for entertainment
  • The naturalness of a schism between popular and classical music
  • The existence of a fixed repertory of ancient masterworks
  • The use of music to divide groups as well as to reinforce the sense of membership within groups.

These are not necessarily found in other cultures. Even the idea that music should be enjoyable to listen to is not even engrained in our own culture.

Glossary

Pitch
- a specific note that we can hear as having a frequency which we can sing back. Most instruments produce pitches; many percussion instruments (i.e drums and bells) do not.
Scale
- an ordered set of pitches, related to a single pitch (the tonic, or root), with each pitch separated by a specific interval. If the scale is ordered by increasing pitches, it is ascending; if it is ordered by descending pitches, it is descending. Because of octave equivalence, scales are usually limited to one octave.
While many different methods of organising pitches into scales are possible, by far the most common scale is the major scale. The major scale uses a particular pattern of (whole) tone (T) and semitone (S) intervals:
T T S T T T S
The same scale can begin on different pitches, using the same interval pattern.
In this diagram, the piano keyboard's white and black notes are indicated in the white and dark columns. Scale beginning on different pitches (listed at left) are given, with the number 1 indicating the first pitch of that scale.
Although we can speak of intervals in general, we often speak of them in relation to a scale, specifically their distance from the tonic/root. Thus, the second pitch after the tonic has an interval of a second (between that pitch and the tonic), the third note after the tonic has an interval of a third, etc.
